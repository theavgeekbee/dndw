what's wrong with contentkeeper?

Hot take: I actually like the concept of ContentKeeper.

At the risk of sounding like a teacher's pet, there are too many absolute bums in almost all of my classes to justify relinquishing web filters.

However, like most things I write about on dndw, this is good in theory, absolutely ATROCIOUS execution.

chapter 1: disillusionment

"The Party told you to reject the evidence of your eyes and ears. It was their final, most essential command."
- George Orwell, 1984

Content Keeper uses DPI, or Deep Packet Inspection, along with SSL inspection to monitor network traffic. Coincidentally, it's also what the Great Firewall of China ↗ uses.

With that last sentence, you can immediately tell that Impero Software is trying WAYYY to hard on this product. I mean, these are literal children trying to play Slope 4 after class we're talking about.

Okay, maybe they just want to keep up the facade of ultra-impenetrability to sell more. I mean, investors want to see state of the sart security or none at all.

Either way, this is a MASSIVE privacy violation (which, by the way, is a human right ↗).

chapter 3: my concerns

I'm going to be real with you, I am nowhere near being a political analyst. I'm just a high school student, so chances are, a lot of chapter 3 might be wrong. But if there's one thing I understand, it is cybersecurity. And there is no arguing with this:

When you look at software through the lens of malicious until proven otherwise (which you SHOULD be doing), closed source software seems to be inherently unsafe. There's no way for you to audit the code, ensure it's integrity, or patch vulnerabilities. It's esssentially a big game of trust me, bro.

It's also very naive to assert that you have zero-day vulnerability protection and leave it at that. You ALWAYS look at security through a lens of, "if things go bad, what can I do"? In this case, if an actual zero-day that affects their tool directly occurs, we have no way of knowing or mitigating that.

tldr; DPI bad because of privacy concerns and cybersecurity vulnerabilities
